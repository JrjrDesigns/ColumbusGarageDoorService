What affects the price

Spring size and wire gauge

Springs are sized to the weight of the door. A heavy insulated double-car door uses a different spring than a lightweight single-car door. Heavier springs cost more. When we diagnose a spring failure, we measure the existing spring and spec the replacement accordingly.

Whether you replace one spring or both

Replacing both torsion springs at once costs somewhat more than replacing one, but significantly less than two separate service calls. If one spring has failed after 10,000 cycles, the second one has also been through 10,000 cycles. We recommend replacing both and explain why — but the choice is yours.

Opener age and parts availability

Older openers from discontinued product lines can be expensive to repair if parts are scarce. At some point, a new opener is more cost-effective. We'll tell you where the line is.

"The question we hear most is whether to repair or replace a spring that's 'only' two or three years old. A spring that fails in under 5,000 cycles is usually a quality issue — builder-grade springs are often undersized for the door they're on. In those cases, we recommend upgrading to a higher-cycle spring rather than replacing like-for-like. The price difference is $40–$80 and the spring will last significantly longer."

No. No trip charge on repairs in Columbus and Franklin County. You pay for the work, not the drive.

No. Emergency and after-hours calls are priced the same as regular service. We don't add a premium for inconvenient timing.

We warranty parts and labor. High-cycle springs come with their own cycle rating warranty. Ask us about specific warranty terms when you call.
